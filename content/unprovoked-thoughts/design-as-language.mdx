There's a persistent myth in our industry that design is about aesthetics. That it's about choosing the right color, the right font, the right spacing. And yes, those things matter. But they're **vocabulary**, not **fluency**.

## The Grammar of Interfaces

Every interface tells a story. The hierarchy of elements establishes *importance*. The spacing between them creates *rhythm*. The transitions between states communicate *time*.

> Design is not how it looks. Design is how it works. — Steve Jobs

But I'd push further: design is not just how it works. Design is how it *communicates*. Every pixel is a word. Every interaction is a sentence. Every user flow is a narrative.

## Why This Matters

When we treat design as a language, several things shift:

- **Consistency becomes grammar** — not arbitrary rules, but shared understanding
- **Innovation becomes poetry** — breaking rules intentionally for expressive effect
- **Accessibility becomes translation** — making the same message understandable across contexts
- **Design systems become dictionaries** — living references, not rigid mandates

### The Vocabulary Problem

Most junior designers have great vocabulary. They know the latest Figma plugins, the trendiest gradients, the most Instagram-worthy layouts. But vocabulary without grammar produces gibberish.

```javascript
// Bad design "grammar"
const page = {
  hero: "LOOK AT ME",
  content: "also look at me",
  sidebar: "ME TOO",
  footer: "AND ME"
};

// Good design "grammar"
const page = {
  hero: "Here's the one thing that matters",
  content: "Here's the context you need",
  sidebar: "Here's where to go next",
  footer: "Here's where you've been"
};
```

## Learning to Write, Not Just Spell

The next time you open Figma, don't ask "does this look good?" Ask:

1. **What is this page saying?** — Can you articulate it in one sentence?
2. **Is the hierarchy clear?** — If you squint, do the important things stand out?
3. **Does the flow have rhythm?** — Are there moments of tension and release?
4. **Is the tone consistent?** — Does the playful button match the serious headline?

Design fluency isn't learned in a bootcamp. It's earned through years of reading great interfaces, writing terrible ones, and slowly developing an ear for what *sounds right*.
